Introduction

To many jellyfish are mysterious flowing ghosts of the ocean with a seemingly celestial glow. While many view these “ghosts” as all animals within the Cnidaria phylum, true jellyfish are defined as animals in the Scyphoza class.



Basic Anatomy

Animals in the Scyphoza category usually live most of their lives in the iconic “umbrella-shaped” (and occasionally cube-shaped) medusa form. There is however a second distinct form that these Scyphoza and all cnidarians share called polyp. Polyps can be thought of as the upside-down version of a medusa. One end of the polyp’s cylindrical form is attached to a solid surface while the other end (facing outwards) is a mouth surrounded by tentacles. Some species of Cnidaria even experience both phases of anatomy starting off as polyps that separate into a stack of disks that break off into multiple jellyfish in a medusa form.




JellyFish Type based off Cnidarian Order

In this guide I have categorized jellyfish based off their taxonomical order. If we narrow down from the Cinidaria phylum to the their six classes we still see significant variation between species. Below I have listed the orders of jellyfish seen in the 75 species documented here along with their characteristics. I have replaced these orders in this guide with their defining characteristics for simplicity’s sake.

Coronatae- Covers mostly deep-sea species with a crown-shaped medusa body and large tentacles. Defined here as crown jellyfish.

Stauromedusae- Sellie, polyps that are sometimes goblet or wine-glass shaped. Defined here as stemmed jellyfish.

Rhizostomeae- Bell-shaped jellies possessing only fuzed tentacles that obstruct the mouth giving a ruffled appearance. Defined here as fused jellyfish.

Lobata- Comb jellies constituting of eight comb rows lined with cilia (short hair-like structures) possessing two lobes extending from the mouth. Defined here as lobed comb jellyfish.

Cydippida- Comb jellies constituting of eight comb rows lined with beating cilia that have tentilla (minature tentacles) attached to their regular tentacles and are often round/ovular in shape. Defined here as rounded comb jellyfish.

Beroida- Comb jellies without tentacles consisting of a sack-like body with a singular oral cavity that functions much like a zipper that forms a temporary seal when not devouring prey. Defined here as Zip-lock jellyfish.

Cestida- Flattened belt-shaped comb jellies consisting of only four comb rows lined with cilia. Defined here as belt jellyfish.

Carybdeida- Jellyfish with a cubic-shaped medusa structure and unbranched tentacles. Defined here as unbranched box jellyfish.

Chirodropida - Jelly with a cubic-shaped medusa structure and branched tentacles. Defined here as branch box jellies.

Siphonophora- Organisms consisting of multiple medusa and polyp species making them colonial organisms. Defined here as complex colonial jellyfish.

Semaeostomeae- Larger jellyfish with an oral cavity stretching to all four of their frilly oral arms/tentacles. Defined here as flag mouth jellyfish.

Trachymedusae- unlike other medusa shape organisms, trachymedusaes do not have are lobes (oral arms) attached to their bell-shaped edge. This order instead has a thickened tissue ring of nemotocysts (sting cells) over and edge lined with hollowed tentacles. Defined here as ringed jellyfish.

Leptothecata- Jellies with polyps that live in colonies with their feeding polyps (referred to as hydranths) inside hydrotheca (cup-like structures). These polyps are covered in a thecate layer (chintinous protective layer) that can retract into hydrotheca. These polyps produce gonophores (the jellyfish reproductive organ) that develop into either medusae or other gonads(reproductive organ that produces sperms or eggs). These jellies have a strong colonial architecture in their polyps stage that effects specie branching and Medusae location. Defined here as chitinous colonial jellyfish.

Limnomedusae- Medusae forms are very similar to Leptothecata however the polyps can live in sessil singular groupings as well as colonial groupings. These polyps are also notably more vulnerable as they do not possess a thecate layer/chintinous exoskeleton. Defined here as non-chitinous colonial jellyfish.

Anthoathecata- Organisms in a medusae form that first go through a solitary colonial polyp phase. They don’t possess statocysts (balance receptors) and possess gonads(reproductive organs) on the edge of their bell as apposed to on the bell in other Hydrozoan orders. Most jellyfish in this order capture prey by discharging structures that stick and immobilize their prey. Defined here as harpoon jellyfish.

As we can see these differing jelly types have varying anatomy that affects their way of life.
